# Org Market Baseline

Date: 2026-03-10
Purpose: anchor the org model in what real coaching businesses already expect from the market.

## What The Market Is Actually Selling

Across current official product pages, the pattern is consistent:

- one org account owns the operating system
- staff roles and permissions are first-class
- directors and owners can oversee coaches
- sessions, registrations, schedules, and communications are centralized
- money is visible at org level, not only coach level
- compliance and eligibility are part of the platform promise

This is visible in official positioning from:

- PlayMetrics: director oversight, practice plans, season plans, and coach compliance with club standards
- TeamSnap: registration, rostering, scheduling, communications, and finance tooling for organizations
- SportsEngine: registration, payment collection, rosters, reporting, and coach/background-check workflows
- Spond Club: recurring payments, group-targeted billing, outstanding payment tracking, and reporting
- Upper Hand: staff management, permissions, payroll reporting, and facility/resource scheduling

## Minimum Believable Org Capabilities

If Clubroom wants to support businesses like `Johnny's Coaching LTD`, the org layer needs these capabilities to feel commercially real:

1. Staff hierarchy
   - owner, ops/admin, head coach/director, coach, assistant
2. Org-owned scheduling
   - sessions can belong to the organization, not only to an individual coach
3. Coach assignment and reassignment
   - an owner or director can assign work and cover absences
4. Org-wide calendar visibility
   - someone above coach level can see delivery load across staff
5. Org-owned financial view
   - revenue, outstanding balances, payouts, and margin are visible to the business
6. Parent-facing clarity
   - families understand whether they are booking a coach directly or booking through an organization
7. Permissioned access
   - assistants do not see the same data as owners
8. Compliance and trust controls
   - role-sensitive access to medical flags, safeguarding issues, and staff eligibility

## What Competitors Imply About Buying Criteria

The buying center is not only the individual coach.

The likely buyers and champions are:

- solo coaches who want a better operating system
- small owner-led coaching companies
- head coaches or directors running multi-coach programs
- club admins who need scheduling, payments, and parent communication in one place

For those buyers, the core questions are:

1. Can I control who sees what?
2. Can I see what my staff are doing?
3. Can I move sessions around when staffing changes?
4. Can I understand the money at business level?
5. Can parents trust the org relationship and chain of responsibility?

## What This Means For Clubroom

Clubroom can still preserve its marketplace and development strengths, but the org layer has to stop reading like an afterthought.

Without a first-class org model:

- multi-coach companies will not trust the platform
- finance and payout logic will stay muddy
- staff visibility will stay inconsistent
- club versus academy language will keep leaking into UX
- families will not have a clean answer to who owns the relationship

## Competitive Positioning Implication

Clubroom does not need to copy every incumbent workflow.

It does need to match the market on the fundamentals:

- org hierarchy
- permissions
- assignment
- centralized schedule visibility
- centralized money visibility
- trust and compliance ownership

The differentiator can then remain:

- stronger coach development tooling
- better parent and athlete progress surfaces
- better football-specific session and objective workflows

## Planning Consequence

The org model should not be treated as a brand choice between `club` and `academy`.

It is a platform capability question:

- who owns the work
- who supervises the work
- who gets paid
- who can intervene
- who carries the trust obligation
